# AC Strange Noise

## Overview
Unusual sounds from indoor or outdoor AC unit during operation. Common sounds include buzzing, grinding, hissing, rattling, or squealing. Indicates loose components, mechanical wear, refrigerant flow issues, or electrical problems. Noise severity may indicate urgency of repair.

## Symptoms
- Buzzing sound from indoor/outdoor unit
- Grinding or mechanical grinding noise during operation
- High-pitched squealing from compressor area
- Hissing from refrigerant lines
- Rattling of indoor/outdoor unit housing
- Clicking sound before compressor starts (normal vs. excessive)
- Vibration noise from ductwork or mounting brackets
- Noise increases with AC runtime

## Possible Causes
**Loose Compressor Bolts**: Compressor vibrates excessively if mounting bolts are loose. Causes rattling and grinding sound.

**Worn Bearings**: Compressor, fan motor, or indoor blower bearings wear over years. Friction produces grinding/squealing sound.

**Loose Components in Outdoor Unit**: Shroud, grill, or internal baffles vibrate and rattle. May be debris rattling inside.

**Refrigerant Flow Noise**: High-speed refrigerant through narrow valve or expansion point creates hissing/whooshing.

**Fan Blade Damage**: Outdoor fan blade cracked, bent, or off-center. Strikes shroud or frame. Produces loud clicking/grinding.

**Electrical Contactor Chatter**: Magnetic contactor cycling rapidly (welded contacts, low voltage). Produces buzzing sound.

**Faulty Capacitor**: Capacitor failing creates buzzing as it tries to energize/de-energize repeatedly.

**Motor Bearing Wear**: Indoor blower motor or outdoor fan motor has worn bearings. High-pitched squeal or grinding on startup.

## DIY Troubleshooting Steps

1. **Identify noise source**: Determine if noise comes from indoor unit, outdoor unit, or ductwork. Listen carefully with AC running.

2. **Check outdoor unit**: Is outdoor fan running and hitting something? Watch fan blade from safe distance. Debris inside? Can it be removed with hand (after ensuring unit is off)?

3. **Inspect visible bolts**: On outdoor compressor, check if mounting bolts are visible and tight. Do not attempt to tighten (risk of mechanical damage). Note if bolts appear loose.

4. **Listen for pattern**: Does noise occur throughout runtime or only at startup/shutdown? Startup noise is often compressor valve opening (normal).

5. **Check ductwork**: If central AC, rattle may come from flexible ducts. Check for loose clamps or collapsed sections.

6. **Measure noise level**: Use phone decibel meter app. Normal AC: 70-75 dB. Loud noise: >85 dB indicates problem.

7. **Try turning off dehumidify mode**: Some ACs have dehumidify-only cycles that cause different motor speeds. Switch to cooling mode and observe if noise changes.

8. **Feel for vibration**: Outdoor unit should vibrate normally. Excessive vibration suggests loose bolts or damaged components.

## Safety Warnings
**Electrical Hazard**: Buzzing often indicates electrical contactor chatter or capacitor failure. Do not attempt to tighten or adjust internal electrical components.

**Mechanical Hazard**: Grinding noise may indicate compressor damage. Running compressor with internal damage can cause complete failure and rupture, releasing refrigerant and oil at high pressure.

**Refrigerant**: Hissing indicates refrigerant leak. Do not approach hissing area. Refrigerant can cause frostbite.

## When To Call A Technician
- Grinding or squealing from compressor
- Buzzing that doesn't stop when AC cycles off
- Hissing sound (refrigerant leak suspected)
- Fan blade striking shroud (clicking/grinding)
- Noise accompanied by reduced cooling
- Any burning smell with noise
- Outdoor unit severely vibrating
- Noise increasing in intensity over days

## Preventive Maintenance
- Have annual professional inspection to identify loose bolts early
- Monitor AC unit during operation for changes in noise pattern
- Ensure outdoor unit is mounted on proper rubber feet/vibration isolators
- Keep outdoor unit clean (dust can rattle when loose)
- Check that ductwork clamps are tight (tighten if loose and accessible)
- Use 5-7 amp fuse for outdoor unit to prevent rapid contactor cycling
- Install noise-dampening shroud around outdoor unit if severe vibration
