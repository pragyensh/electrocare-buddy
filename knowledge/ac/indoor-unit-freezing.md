# AC Indoor Unit Freezing

## Overview
Indoor evaporator coil develops frost or ice layer that blocks airflow. Initially appears as white frost, progresses to solid ice if untreated. Prevents cooling, reduces airflow, and can damage compressor if ice breaks off into refrigerant lines. Requires immediate attention.

## Symptoms
- White frost visible on indoor coil or outlet
- Weak airflow or complete blockage from vents
- No cooling despite compressor running
- Ice forming around drainage area
- Water dripping inside room during thaw cycle
- Unit automatically shutting off after 5-10 minutes
- Unusual smell (coil thawing)
- Hissing or gurgling from unit

## Possible Causes
**Low Refrigerant Charge**: Refrigerant pressure too low, causing evaporator temperature to drop below freezing point (0°C or below). Most common cause.

**Restricted Airflow**: Dirty air filter or blocked return air vent prevents warm room air from passing over coil. Coil temperature drops excessively.

**Thermostat Sensor Malfunction**: Sensor cannot accurately measure coil/air temperature. Thermostat keeps cooling even as coil freezes.

**Faulty Expansion Valve**: Valve sticks in open position, allowing excessive refrigerant into coil, dropping pressure and temperature.

**Defrost Cycle Failure**: AC programmed defrost cycle malfunctions. Compressor runs continuously without periodic shutdown to allow thaw.

**Outdoor Temperature Too Low**: Running AC when outdoor temperature below 16-18°C or on full-blast in winter mode causes freezing.

**Blower Motor Failure**: Indoor fan not running or running slowly, reducing airflow over coil.

## DIY Troubleshooting Steps

1. **Stop the AC immediately**: Turn off unit. Continuing operation with frozen coil risks compressor damage.

2. **Allow thaw**: Wait 3-4 hours with fan running (if available) or windows open. Do not pour hot water on coil (thermal shock damage risk).

3. **Replace air filter**: Open indoor unit, check filter. If clogged, replace with new filter. Dirty filter is frequent cause of freezing.

4. **Check return air**: Ensure return air vents are not blocked by furniture, curtains, or closed doors.

5. **Check outdoor temperature**: If outside is cold (<16°C), running AC is problematic. Defrost cycle designed for normal outdoor temps.

6. **Test thermostat**: After thaw, set temperature to 1-2°C above room temperature. Unit should cool gradually without extreme coil chilling. If still freezes within 30 minutes, refrigerant charge is likely low.

7. **Inspect for water**: Thawing ice produces water. Ensure it drains properly. Blocked drain backing up water may have caused freezing in first place.

8. **Listen for defrost cycle**: After AC runs for 20 minutes, it should briefly reverse (compressor stops, fan may reverse or stop). Defrost cycle prevents ice buildup. If no defrost sounds, cycle is failed.

## Safety Warnings
**Ice Breaking**: Large ice chunks dislodging from coil can damage compressor if they enter refrigerant lines. Never use sharp tools to dislodge ice.

**Thermal Shock**: Pouring hot water on frozen coil causes sudden temperature change, potentially cracking the copper coil. Allow natural thaw only.

**Electrical**: Do not attempt to inspect defrost sensor/heating element inside unit. High-voltage components present.

**Flooding**: Melting ice produces large water volume. Ensure drain is clear and water can exit safely without flooding interior.

## When To Call A Technician
- Ice returns within 30 minutes of thaw
- Defrost cycle not activating (technician can test)
- Refrigerant leak suspected (frost pattern asymmetric or excessive)
- Expansion valve suspected (coil too cold even with normal airflow)
- Blower motor not running
- Thermostat sensor replacement needed
- Compressor damage suspected (grinding noise during initial thaw)

## Preventive Maintenance
- Replace air filter every 15-20 days during cooling season
- Ensure return air vents are always open
- Do not use AC when outdoor temperature is below 16°C
- Do not set temperature too low on cool nights (set to 22°C minimum)
- Professional inspection annually before cooling season (verify defrost function)
- Check refrigerant charge annually
- Ensure thermostat is in optimal location (not in direct sunlight or near heat source)
- Monitor first operation after maintenance for ice formation
