# AC Bad Smell

## Overview
Foul, musty odor emanating from AC vents. Smell intensifies when AC first turns on. Caused by mold/mildew growth on evaporator coil, stagnant water in drain line, or bacterial accumulation. Can affect indoor air quality and trigger allergies.

## Symptoms
- Musty/moldy odor when AC starts
- Smell worse after AC sits idle for days
- Odor stronger during humid months
- Reduced air freshness despite temperature control
- Respiratory irritation in sensitive individuals
- Visible black spots on indoor unit air outlet
- Damp smell similar to wet basement

## Possible Causes
**Mold on Evaporator Coil**: Coil collects moisture and dust. Organic matter (pollen, skin cells) feeds mold growth. Black spots visible on coil surface. Spores blow out with air circulation.

**Stagnant Water in Drain Line**: Algae and bacteria grow in warm, stagnant drain tube. Organic decomposition produces foul smell. Especially during humid months when condensation is high.

**Bacterial Growth in Drainage Pan**: Pan under evaporator coil holds standing water if drain is slow or blocked. Anaerobic bacteria produce sulfurous or rotten-egg smell.

**Dead Organism in Unit**: Bird, insect, or rodent died inside outdoor unit or ductwork. Decomposition smell lasting weeks until body dries.

**Dirty Air Filter**: Dust-clogged filter traps moisture. Organic matter ferments, producing smell.

**Ductwork Contamination**: If AC connected to central ducting, contamination upstream (old ductwork, rodent nesting) introduces odors.

## DIY Troubleshooting Steps

1. **Replace air filter immediately**: Dispose of old filter. Install fresh filter. Smell should reduce in 30 minutes if filter was primary cause.

2. **Locate smell source**: Turn off AC. Smell indoor unit air outlet. Is odor very strong directly from outlet or distributed? Strong outlet odor suggests coil/pan issue.

3. **Visual inspection of indoor unit**: Carefully remove front panel. Look at visible portion of evaporator coil. Black/green spots indicate mold. White powder is dust-mold mix.

4. **Check drain pan**: If accessible, look under coil. Water in pan should be clear. Murky/brown water suggests bacterial growth. Smell pan directly—rotten-egg smell confirms bacteria.

5. **Flush drain line**: Use water hose to flush drain line from outdoor end. Foul-smelling water indicates stagnant water with algae/bacteria. Flushing removes some odor.

6. **Improve air circulation**: Ensure return air vents are not blocked. Stagnant air promotes mold. Open windows periodically to refresh indoor air.

7. **Check outdoor unit**: Inspect compressor area and intake for dead animals or debris. Remove if found (use gloves, dispose properly).

8. **Activate deodorizing mode**: Some modern ACs have ionizer or UV lamp. If available, activate for 2 hours to reduce airborne bacteria/mold.

## Safety Warnings
**Mold Spores**: Inhaling mold can trigger asthma, allergies, and respiratory infections. Particularly dangerous for children, elderly, and immunocompromised. Use mask while working near visible mold.

**Drain Line Bacteria**: Some drain bacteria produce endotoxins that irritate respiratory system. Do not inhale drain fumes.

**Refrigerant Exposure**: If you dislodge contamination that enters refrigerant lines, it can damage compressor. Avoid aggressive cleaning of internal coil.

## When To Call A Technician
- Visible mold on evaporator coil
- Persistent foul smell despite filter replacement and drain flushing
- Black spots/growth not removable by flushing
- Dead animal suspected inside unit
- Smell accompanied by respiratory symptoms
- Drainage pan bacterial growth
- Smell indicates refrigerant leak (sweet/sickly smell)

## Preventive Maintenance
- Replace air filter every 15-20 days during cooling season
- Clean drain line monthly with water or dilute bleach (1:10 ratio)
- Run AC in dry mode for 30 minutes before shutdown to dry coil
- Use dehumidifier to keep indoor humidity below 55%
- Install UV lamp in indoor unit (proven mold inhibitor)
- Add algaecide tablets to drain line quarterly
- Ensure outdoor unit receives sunlight (UV naturally inhibits mold)
- Keep indoor unit temperature setting consistent (rapid cycling creates condensation)
